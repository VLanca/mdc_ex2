simulação & manipulação

Video Games and Computer Holding Power, de Sherry Turkle é iniciada uma discussão sobre o computador como cultura, e a simulação. É abordado as diferenças de videojogos e jogos mecânicos. Turkle dá o exemplo do jogo
pinball
em que os elementos do jogo dependam das regras do mundo real, ou seja, a bola tem que obedecer às leis da gravidade. No entanto, nos videojogos essa condição é simulada, o que importa é o código e não se existe alguma lei da física. A existência de gravidade do videojogo depende da intenção do programador (pp.502) [5]. Nos jogos mecânicos também é de salientar que nem toda a máquina responderá da mesma forma, a mesma lógica para os videojogos que cada jogador responderá ao jogo de maneiras diferentes.


“[…]with neither color nor detail, it was less a spaceship than a spaceship ideogram. Just like the square ball in Pong, the spaceship was there to serve as a “marker.”[5]
“A white spot acted as a Ping-Pong ball, which ricocheted off the wall and had to be hit back by the movable white rectangle. The user developed skill involving speed and accuracy in placement of the “paddle” to keep the increasingly speedy ball from getting by, while the speaker emitted a ponging sound when the ball bounced.”[6]

“New programming techniques offer the hope of creating characters who have more specificc and interesting personalities than the monsters in Pac-Man so that players’ interactions with them may feel more like a social encounter and less like controlling a pinball.”[5]
“Consider Pac-Man, the first game to be acknowledged as part of the national culture” (...) The player controls Pac-Man, or, as children usually express it, “You are zac-Man.” Your job is to eat the food and avoid being eaten by the monsters.”[5]
“The designers of these games have provided stimulating entertainment, a challenge for novices and experts, and many intriguing lessons in the human factors of interface design—somehow they have found a way to get people to put coins into the sides of computers.”[6]
